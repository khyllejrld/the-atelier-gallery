The French Impressionist  /  The Man Simply Referred to as "Vincent"

Biography

Claude Monet

Let's start with the man Impressionism is basically named after. Monet grew up in Le Havre sketching caricatures for pocket money, long before he ever picked up a landscape brush — a funny start for someone who'd go on to spend decades painting the same pond.

He helped organize the 1874 exhibition that gave the whole movement its name, after a critic mocked one of his paintings, Impression, Sunrise, as unfinished and sloppy. The name stuck, and so did Monet's habit: paint the same subject again and again — haystacks, poplar trees, the front of Rouen Cathedral — and let the shifting light do the storytelling.

By the time he settled at Giverny, he'd built himself a water garden purely so he'd never run out of a subject worth returning to. He kept painting it, in versions that grew looser and more abstract, right up until his eyesight failed and his life ended in 1926.

Vincent van Gogh

Van Gogh's story is a much shorter one. He grew up in the Netherlands and tried a couple of other careers first — art dealing, then training for the ministry — before painting finally stuck, and by then he didn't have much time to waste.

Moving to Paris in 1886 put him in the same rooms as the Impressionists, and their color rubbed off on him fast. Looking for cheaper light and more of it, he headed south to Arles, where he painted at a pace that's hard to picture: Sunflowers, Café Terrace at Night, and dozens more, all within about a year.

It was also in Arles that his mental health began to break down, badly enough that he checked himself into the asylum at Saint-Rémy-de-Provence. Even there, he kept painting — The Starry Night came from that period, worked partly from memory and partly from the view outside his window.

He died in 1890, only thirty-seven, having sold almost nothing in his lifetime. It was his brother Theo, an art dealer, who spent the next two years laying the groundwork for the recognition Van Gogh never got to see.
