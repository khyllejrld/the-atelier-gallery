Biography

Claude Monet

Born in Paris and raised in Le Havre, Monet began as a caricaturist before turning to landscape painting outdoors, a practice that would define Impressionism. He co-founded the movement's first group exhibition in 1874, where the term "Impressionism" was coined in response to his painting Impression, Sunrise.

Working largely en plein air, Monet returned again and again to the same subjects, haystacks, poplars, the façade of Rouen Cathedral, recording how a single scene shifted under changing light, weather, and season.

In his later years at Giverny, he focused almost entirely on the water garden he built himself, producing the Water Lilies series that occupied him until his death in 1926. Though his eyesight declined sharply in his final decade, he kept painting, and the late Water Lilies canvases grew looser and more abstract, foreshadowing developments in painting that would come long after him.

Vincent van Gogh

Born in Groot-Zundert in the Netherlands, van Gogh came to painting relatively late, after failed attempts at careers in art dealing and the ministry. He moved to Paris in 1886, where exposure to Impressionist and Neo-Impressionist painters pushed his palette toward brighter, more saturated color.

Seeking cheaper light and a subject of his own, he relocated to Arles in the south of France, where he painted with extraordinary speed and intensity, producing Sunflowers, Café Terrace at Night, and dozens of other canvases in little more than a year.

His mental health deteriorated during this period, culminating in a breakdown that led him to voluntarily enter the asylum at Saint-Rémy-de-Provence, where he continued to paint, including The Starry Night, working from memory and from the view outside his window.

Van Gogh sold almost nothing during his lifetime and died in 1890 at the age of thirty-seven, two years before his brother Theo, an art dealer, began the effort that would eventually bring his work international recognition.
